## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Henry Boot is a UK land-led property group (Hallam Land, Stonebridge Homes, commercial development) trading at a deep discount to book: P/B ~0.5, P/E ~10, dividend yield ~4.8%. The quantitative screen flags cheapness, dividend quality, and balance-sheet resilience (current ratio ~4.9), with a stable buy signal over five weeks. The investment case rests on a large land bank and development pipeline — including the Golden Valley cyber hub — versus cyclical UK housing/planning risk and weak reported ROE (~4%). Key debate: whether NAV and land-sale optionality are mispriced, or whether negative free cash flow, rising net debt, and management transition cap upside.

## INVESTMENT THESIS

For a value investor, BOOT screens as a classic asset play on UK residential and commercial land rather than a pure housebuilder. Seven of 22 models pass, spanning Graham Defensive, Schloss low P/B, earnings yield, low P/E plus high yield, dividend growth, composite value, and financial health — three of four factor families (cheapness, dividend, risk). At P/B 0.52 and P/E 10.2, the market prices equity well below Yahoo-reported book value of ~£423m (FY25) and net assets heavily weighted to inventory (~£368m) and investment property (~£95m).

Business quality is mixed but defensible: long operating history, family-influenced stewardship, recurring land sales, and a pivot toward higher-margin prime developments and premium homes (per Mar 2026 earnings commentary). Portfolio simplification — construction division MBO (~£4m, completed Jan 2026) and asset recycling — supports a capital-light, land-bank model. Dividend continuity (~£10.5m paid in FY25) aligns with the dividend-growth screen pass.

The screen’s buy signal is not a quality-growth call: ROE is only 4.3% and revenue has contracted post-disposal. The thesis is that tangible NAV, planning gains on strategic sites (Golden Valley, Arcadis letting), and a 4.8% yield offer asymmetric upside if UK planning and housing demand stabilise. Conviction from the screen is moderate (38%) but persistent.

## RISKS AND RED FLAGS

**Cyclical and operational**
- UK housebuilding and land markets remain planning- and rate-sensitive; revenue and operating profit are on a multi-year downtrend (Yahoo).
- Inventory-heavy balance sheet (£368m) creates mark-to-market and timing risk if land values or absorption slow.
- Negative FY25 free cash flow and rising net debt (+£46m) increase refinancing sensitivity; cash cover is thin (£8.4m vs £116m debt).

**Strategic / portfolio**
- Construction division sale reduces diversification and near-term revenue; integration of any Care REIT transaction (Mar 2025 RNS headline) adds execution and sector (care homes/REIT) risk — terms not verified without filing body.
- Golden Valley (~£1bn project; phase 1 funding secured Jul 2026) is long-dated (completion cited early 2028) and capital-intensive.

**Governance and management**
- **CEO succession update** (12 Jun 2026) and **directorate change** (3 Jun 2026) introduce transition risk during a leverage uptick.
- Positive signal: **CEO share purchase** (16 Jun 2026) and **buy-back extension** (RNS, Jul 2026) suggest insider confidence, but not a substitute for audited disclosure.

**Pension and regulatory**
- Defined benefit pension exposure improved but warrants monitoring in annual report (not available).
- Investors’ Chronicle piece, “Stay cautious with this lowly rated property stock” (23 Sep 2025), flags scepticism on property cyclicality — aligned with low ROE.
