## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Central Asia Metals is an AIM-listed base-materials producer (Kaz copper, North Macedonia zinc–lead at Sasa) that the quantitative screen flags as a buy on cheapness and balance-sheet quality: P/B ~1.1, FCF yield ~26%, dividend yield ~8.7%, and negligible leverage (D/E ~1%). The valuation hook is a cash-generative operator trading near tangible book after a sharp reported loss driven by a Sasa life-of-mine impairment (news cites up to US$120m), not by operating collapse—underlying operating profit and FCF remained positive in FY2025 on Yahoo data. The central debate is whether asset write-downs and a shorter Sasa reserve base are a one-off reset versus a structural erosion of NAV, and whether management’s pivot into growth M&A (Cygnus Metals, A$232m) redeploys surplus capital or dilutes the capital-return story. Macro context is unavailable in source files and is not used here.

## INVESTMENT THESIS

For a value investor, CAML presents a classic “bad GAAP, good cash” profile that aligns with the screen’s cheapness and risk families. Six of 22 models pass—Schloss Low P/B, FCF Yield, Magic Formula, Acquirer’s Multiple, Composite Value, and Financial Health—with a composite score of 0.80 versus 0.68 sector-relative and high data quality (18/20 metrics). The business generates recurring operating cash (FY2025 operating cash flow US$63.7m per Yahoo) and free cash flow (US$42.9m) despite a reported net loss of US$75.1m, because the loss is dominated by non-cash write-offs (unusual items US$119.2m; normalized net income ~US$14.8m). Balance-sheet strength supports the risk bucket: cash US$79.7m, total debt US$2.2m, working capital US$73.9m—consistent with the screen’s sub-50% D/E pass at ~1%. Revenue has grown three years running (US$203.5m → US$214.4m → US$229.9m), suggesting operational scale intact even as normalized EBITDA eased (US$106.2m to US$93.7m). The negative ROE (-23.4%) reflects the impairment year rather than a levered equity wipe-out. Capital allocation—dividends, buybacks, and now Cygnus—offers optional upside if acquisitions are disciplined; the screen’s buy signal is therefore grounded in tangible asset backing and cash yield, not reported earnings momentum.

## RISKS AND RED FLAGS

**Reserve and asset-quality risk (material):** News reports (3 March 2026) that a revised Sasa life-of-mine plan triggered a non-cash impairment of up to US$120m and a ~20% share-price fall. Yahoo FY2025 data corroborates a large write-off. This raises reserve-replacement and NAV durability questions beyond what a P/B screen captures.

**Earnings vs cash divergence:** Reported ROE is deeply negative (-23.4% per screen) while FCF is strong—a value trap risk if impairments recur or if normalized EBITDA continues to drift lower (US$106m → US$94m).

**M&A and capital allocation:** Proposed acquisition of Cygnus Metals for A$232m (2 June 2026, share-exchange structure per news) expands into Canadian copper—execution, geological, and integration risk. Prior interest in New World Resources (July 2025 update) signals an acquisitive pivot that may compete with dividends/buybacks. March 2026 **Proposed Share Premium Cancellation** suggests balance-sheet engineering tied to capital structure—details unavailable without filing bodies.

**Dividend trajectory:** Dividends were cut in September 2025 (news: “CAML cuts dividend in buyout push”; “Central Asia Metals tightens dividend as profits slide”). FY2025 cash dividends (US$31.4m) fell vs FY2024 (US$40.9m). High trailing yield (8.7%) may not be sustainable if payout policy prioritises M&A.

**Geographic and commodity cyclicality:** Operations in Kazakhstan and North Macedonia carry jurisdiction, permitting, and geopolitical exposure; revenues are commodity-linked (copper, zinc, lead). No primary filing language on hedging or offtake is available here.

**Governance / pension:** Balance-sheet pension and provision lines (~US$52m combined long-term provisions plus pension) imply closure-cost obligations. Board changes (Non-Executive Director changes, 20 August 2025) noted in news. Institutional ownership is high (~80% per January 2026 news)—liquidity and crowded exit risk on negative surprises.

**Primary filing gaps:** Absence of going-concern, covenant, or litigation disclosures from RNS bodies is a research red flag in itself; these must be read directly from the Annual Report and Half-year Report before sizing risk.
