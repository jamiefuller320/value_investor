**EXECUTIVE SUMMARY**

Orora Limited is an Australasian packaging group (beverage cans, closures and related formats) that has undergone a major portfolio reshaping, including the 2023–24 Saverglass acquisition and a substantial FY2025 divestiture that generated roughly A$907m in discontinued-operation gains and sharply reduced leverage. The quantitative screen rates the stock a **strong buy** on cheapness (P/B 0.6, P/E 14.3), a 7.0% dividend yield, modest gearing (D/E ~29%), and FCF-yield criteria, with nine of 22 models passing across all four value families. The central debate is whether post-divestiture balance-sheet strength and asset backing justify accumulation, or whether weak continuing ROE (4.4%), sub-FCF dividend cover, and an April 2026 earnings reset driven by Middle East disruption at Saverglass signal a value trap. **No primary ASX annual or interim filing bodies are available in the research library** (regime: unsupported); all financial figures below fall back to Yahoo.

---

**INVESTMENT THESIS**

For a value investor, Orora presents a classic post-transformation setup: the share price screens deep relative to book (P/B 0.64, passing Schloss Low P/B and Deep Value), offers an elevated yield (7.0%, passing High Dividend Yield and Dividend Growth), and carries de-risked leverage after FY2025 net debt fell from A$1.72bn to A$228m (Yahoo). The screen composite score of 94% (sector-relative 85%) with families spanning cheapness, dividend, GARP and risk suggests the market is pricing structural uncertainty rather than imminent insolvency — consistent with Financial Health and FCF Yield passes. Continuing operations, however, are thin: FY2025 continuing net income was only A$66m on A$2.9bn equity (ROE ~4.4%), which the screen captures but does not fully penalise. The bull case rests on (1) simplified, lower-debt core packaging earnings, (2) capital return (A$127m buyback and sustained dividends in FY2025), and (3) multiple mean-reversion if cans growth offsets glass weakness. The bear case — earnings quality, geopolitical glass exposure, and dividend/FCF mismatch — is not reflected in cheapness metrics alone. Net: the screen correctly identifies **asset-backed cheapness and income**, but business-quality signals are mixed; conviction overlay should be moderate, not full.

**RISKS AND RED FLAGS**

1. **Earnings reset and cyclicality** — April 2026 profit-guidance cut (Saverglass FY26 underlying EBIT trimmed to €63–68m) drove a 19% share-price fall to a 12-year low. Continuing ROE of 4.4% suggests the market may be discounting sustained weak returns, not just temporary noise.

2. **Geopolitical / operational** — Middle East tensions halted production at Saverglass’s Ras Al Khaimah (UAE) facility. Without filing contingency language, exposure size and insurance/recovery cannot be verified; news indicates glass remains a strategic pain point despite cans strength.

3. **M&A and portfolio complexity** — The FY2024 Saverglass acquisition (A$2.16bn) followed by FY2025 large discontinued gain implies a rapid buy-then-sell cycle that raises governance and capital-allocation questions. Integration/disposal gains can obscure underlying packaging economics.

4. **Dividend sustainability** — ~7% yield is attractive on screen, but dividends exceeded FCF in the last two reported years (Yahoo). Without filing dividend-policy statements, franking and payout-ratio commitments are unverified.

5. **Interest and lease burden** — FY2025 interest expense A$66m (down from A$97m) plus capitalised leases A$168m still matter on A$66m continuing NI. Covenant headroom cannot be assessed without annual-report notes (missing).

6. **Intangibles and impairment** — A$1.48bn goodwill/intangibles against A$2.90bn equity; any Saverglass or glass impairment would hit book value and weaken the P/B thesis.

7. **Governance / going concern** — No filing bodies available; going-concern, litigation, or pension disclosures cannot be reviewed. Yahoo shows employee benefit liabilities of A$30m non-current plus A$74m current — immaterial relative to equity but unverified.

8. **Screen blind spots** — Low ROE, earnings volatility, and event-driven earnings are poorly captured by static cheapness/dividend models; screen conviction is only 51% (“building”, two weeks at signal).
