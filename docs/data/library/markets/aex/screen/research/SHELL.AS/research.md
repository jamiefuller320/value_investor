# Shell plc (SHELL.AS) — Research memo

_Version 1 · Updated 2026-07-18T18:21:16.334737+00:00 · Mode: initial_

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Shell plc screens as a **strong buy** on the quantitative value model (composite 79.5%, 10/22 models passed across cheapness, dividend, GARP and risk families), supported by a modest P/E of 13.5, P/B of 1.4, 3.6% yield and conservative leverage (D/E 43%). The investment case rests on a cash-generative integrated energy franchise—anchored in LNG and upstream optionality—returning capital aggressively via dividends and buybacks while the share count shrinks. The central debate is whether normalised earnings have troughed after the post-2022 commodity unwind, or whether structurally lower oil/gas realisations and transition capex will cap returns; governance friction (CEO pay rising as headline profits fell) and Russia-related litigation add non-quantitative noise. Filing body gaps limit first-pass confirmation of balance-sheet contingencies and going-concern language.

## INVESTMENT THESIS
For a value investor, Shell offers a rare combination of **cheapness, income and balance-sheet quality** in a sector where cyclicality often destroys screens. The model passes Graham Enterprising, FCF Yield, Lynch/Neff PEG variants, Dividend Growth, Magic Formula, Acquirer's Multiple, Dreman Contrarian, Composite Value and Financial Health—four of four factor families cleared with 19/20 data quality. That breadth suggests the signal is not a single-ratio artefact.

Business quality supports the screen: Shell remains a top-tier integrated major with scale in LNG (management targets ~5% annual LNG sales growth; expects 60% global LNG demand rise by 2040 per company messaging cited in news), trading and downstream, generating substantial operating cash even as revenues normalise from the 2022 peak. Capital discipline is evident—capex fell to c.$18.9bn in FY2025 (Yahoo fallback) while the group returned c.$8.5bn in dividends and c.$13.9bn in buybacks; diluted average shares fell from 6.36bn (2024) to 5.95bn (2025). ROE of 10.7% is adequate for a capital-intensive cyclical, and net debt of c.$17.1bn against $29.6bn cash (Yahoo, FY2025) leaves headroom. The GARP pass implies the market prices mid-cycle earnings too pessimistically relative to cash-return capacity—a plausible mispricing if LNG volumes and cost savings offset weaker realised prices.

## RISKS AND RED FLAGS
**Cyclical and commodity:** Revenue down c.30% from FY2022 peak; operating income nearly halved. Earnings remain highly sensitive to oil, gas and refining margins—news coverage links share volatility to Middle East conflict and oil price spikes (March–July 2026). Screens may look attractive at a mid-cycle trough but deteriorate quickly in a downturn.

**Capital return vs FCF:** FY2025 distributions exceeded FCF, pushing net debt higher. Sustained payout above trough FCF is a red flag if commodity prices weaken further.

**Russia exposure:** Shell seeks to exit its Russian subsidiary while Moscow pursues compensation claims ("Russia Sues Shell for $1.6Bln Over Unpaid 2022 Gazprom Supplies", The Moscow Times, 25 March 2025; Upstream Online, October 2024). Litigation outcome and stranded-asset risk are unquantified in available sources.

**Governance:** CEO Wael Sawan's pay rose sharply (Reuters/The Guardian, March 2026: c.60% increase to c.£13.8m proposed) amid profit slippage—creates reputational and AGM friction; shareholders rejected a climate activist resolution while re-electing CEO/chairman (EnergyNow.com, 19 May 2026).

**Transition and portfolio:** Shell is reshaping its portfolio—selling India renewables (Sprng Energy), weighing Argentina Vaca Muerta divestment (Shale24, February 2026), exiting Oman blocks—strategy pivot toward LNG/gas and away from some low-return assets carries execution risk. GHG emissions fell 9% in 2025 (Quantum Commodity Intelligence, 12 March 2026) but transition capex and regulatory pressure remain structural overhangs.

**Pensions and provisions:** $7.1bn non-current pension/post-retirement liabilities and $21.4bn long-term provisions (Yahoo)—cannot assess funding status or decommissioning adequacy without filing notes.

**Audit change:** Switch to PwC in 2027 after EY tenure (Stock Titan, 6 February 2026)—routine but worth noting at transition.

**Filing gap:** Absence of primary filing bodies means going-concern, litigation contingencies, and covenant language **cannot be reviewed** in this pass—a material limitation for risk assessment.
