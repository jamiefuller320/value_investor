## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

United Microelectronics Corporation is a Taiwan-listed mature-node wafer foundry trading as a US ADR, currently flagged **buy** by the quantitative screen on cash-flow, GARP, quality, dividend, and balance-sheet metrics. The investment case rests on a **fortress balance sheet** (net cash, low leverage), **normalising free cash flow** after a heavy capex cycle, and **early-cycle recovery** in revenue and quarterly earnings, partially offset by a **multi-year compression** in gross and operating margins from cyclical peak levels. Valuation is not classic deep value on headline multiples (P/E ~34, P/B ~4.2), but screen passes on FCF yield, PEG, and financial health suggest the market still underprices normalised cash generation relative to trough earnings. The central debate is whether specialty-node mix (automotive, connectivity, silicon photonics) and capacity discipline can restore margins, or whether UMC remains a structurally lower-return second-tier foundry versus TSMC and GlobalFoundries in a prolonged mature-node pricing war.

## INVESTMENT THESIS

The quantitative screen rates UMC **buy** (7/22 models passed; composite score 57%; five factor families: cheapness, quality, dividend, GARP, risk). Key pass models include **FCF Yield**, **Lynch PEG**, **Neff PEGY**, **Quality Value**, **Dividend Growth**, **Acquirer's Multiple**, and **Financial Health**, with reported metrics of P/E 34.3, P/B 4.2, dividend yield 1.9%, ROE 12.5%, FCF yield 64.4%, and PEG 0.32. For a value investor, the signal aligns with business quality more than absolute cheapness: UMC combines **net cash of roughly NT$51bn**, **debt/equity near 16%**, sustained **dividend payments** (NT$36–38bn annually in 2024–25), and a **2025 FCF rebound to NT$49.1bn** after capex-heavy, FCF-negative 2023.

Business quality is adequate for a cyclical semi: UMC is a scaled pure-play foundry focused on mature and specialty nodes rather than leading-edge logic, with geographic diversification across Taiwan, Singapore, China (Xiamen, Suzhou), and Japan. Revenue stabilised and ticked up in 2024–25 after the 2023 downturn, and the most recent quarter shows **+3.9% revenue** and sharply higher net income year-on-year (Yahoo quarterly data), consistent with a trough-to-recovery narrative that supports the screen's elevated growth and PEG inputs. The screen's FCF yield appears unusually high and should be treated cautiously—it likely reflects a trailing FCF spike against ADR market cap and possible currency aggregation—but directionally it confirms that **cash generation recovered faster than earnings multiples imply**.

The value hook is **asset-backed cash optionality**: large cash and investment balances, manageable leverage, active **share buybacks** (news: surpassing NT$1.08bn threshold, May 2026), and capital returns that the Dividend Growth and Financial Health models capture. UMC is a buy for investors willing to hold through foundry cyclicality in exchange for balance-sheet protection and improving FCF at a point where earnings remain depressed versus the 2022 peak.

**Revenue** fell sharply from the 2022 cyclical peak (NT$278.7bn) to NT$222.5bn in 2023, then **recovered modestly** to NT$237.6bn by 2025—a shallow U-shape rather than full normalisation. **Profitability continued to deteriorate** through 2025 despite higher revenue: net income fell each year from NT$89.5bn (2022) to NT$40.4bn (2025), driven by **margin compression** (gross margin down ~16 percentage points from peak) and higher operating costs including R&D (NT$17.7bn in 2025 vs NT$13.0bn in 2022).

**Cash flow** tells a more constructive story. FY2023–24 were **capex-intensive** (capital expenditure NT$94.0bn and NT$91.3bn respectively), producing negative or near-zero FCF. FY2025 saw **capex ease to NT$50.7bn** while operating cash flow remained strong at NT$99.9bn, yielding **FCF of NT$49.1bn**—a sharp normalisation that underpins the screen's FCF Yield pass. **Dividends** remained substantial: cash dividends paid of NT$37.6bn (2024) and NT$35.8bn (2025), indicating management prioritises shareholder returns even as reported earnings decline.

## RISKS AND RED FLAGS

**Cyclical and competitive.** Foundry earnings remain **well below the 2022 peak**; gross margins near **29%** versus **45%** at peak suggest pricing and utilisation pressure in mature nodes. UMC competes with TSMC (dominant), GlobalFoundries, SMIC, and others; BofA commentary in the news flow notes **limited TSMC spillover benefit** to UMC (21 Jan 2026). The screen's buy signal may reflect trough-relative metrics that **reverse if the cycle stalls**.

**Geopolitical and China exposure.** UMC operates fabs in **mainland China** (Xiamen, Suzhou per 20-F entity tags). Cross-strait tension, export controls, or local competition pose regulatory and operational risks not captured in quantitative screens.

**Customer concentration.** The 20-F taxonomy includes **CustomerConcentrationRisk** tags for major wafer-fabrication customers (Customer A in 2024–25; Customer B in 2023). Loss or repricing of a key customer would disproportionately affect utilisation.

**Earnings quality.** FY2025 and the latest quarter include **investment gains, equity income, and securities sales** that inflate net income relative to operating profit. Value investors should anchor on **operating cash flow and normalised FCF**, not headline NI.

**Capital intensity.** Capex ran **NT$91–94bn** in 2023–24; while FY2025 capex halved, the 20-F tags reference ongoing **capacity reservations, guarantee deposits, and tool purchases** (news: NT$1bn tool purchase, May 2026). A re-acceleration of capex could again suppress FCF.

**Balance-sheet complexity.** Tags reference **exchangeable bonds**, **embedded derivatives**, **multiple debt tranches** (domestic unsecured bonds maturing 2026–2031), and **secured syndicated facilities**. Covenant or refinancing risk cannot be assessed from the truncated extract, but the **net cash position** mitigates near-term concern.

**Pension and employee obligations.** **Defined benefit plan** and **minimum pension liability** tags appear in both Yahoo balance sheet (minimum pension liabilities ~NT$2.1bn) and 20-F metadata. This is manageable at current scale but adds fixed costs.

**Governance / related parties.** Extensive **related-party and associate investment** tags (Faraday, Unimicron, Novatek, etc.) indicate a complex corporate network; related-party transactions are not quantifiable from available extracts.

**Data and filing gaps.** The inability to read primary filing narratives is itself a research red flag: risk factors, contingencies, and covenant language could not be verified from `filings/bodies/`. Macro context snapshot is **missing**.
