## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Pfizer screens as a strong buy on classic value metrics—P/E 19.2, P/B 1.6, dividend yield 6.9%, debt/equity 72%—after a multi-year de-rating from the COVID revenue peak. The investment case rests on a post-pandemic revenue base stabilising near $63bn, a deep oncology and vaccine pipeline (Seagen, Keytruda alliances, recent PADCEV approval), and income-oriented capital return. The central debate is whether headline cheapness reflects a durable reset or a value trap: normalised earnings are well above reported net income, yet ROE is only 8.3%, tangible book value is negative, and cash dividends materially exceed GAAP earnings. Pipeline execution, patent litigation, IRA pricing pressure, and the $4.7bn cost-realignment programme through 2027 define the risk/reward.

---

## INVESTMENT THESIS

The quantitative screen flags Pfizer across all four factor families—cheapness, dividend, GARP, and risk—with ten passing models including Graham Enterprising, FCF Yield, High Dividend Yield, Magic Formula, and Financial Health. That breadth is unusual for a mega-cap pharma name and reflects a valuation reset: shares are down roughly 22% over five years (per recent coverage), while the business has moved from $101bn revenue (FY2022) to a sub-$64bn run-rate.

For a value investor, the hook is asymmetric: the market prices Pfizer as a declining COVID franchise, yet interim filings show revenue stabilisation—Q1 2026 total revenues of $14.5bn rose 5% year-on-year—and nine-month 2025 net income of $9.4bn exceeded the prior-year comparable by 24%, despite flat top line. Alliance revenues are growing (Q1 2026: $2.3bn vs $2.1bn), reflecting oncology partnerships. The 6.9% yield and sub-25x P/E offer income and rerating optionality if pipeline catalysts (oncology, RSV, obesity via Metsera) convert.

Business quality is mixed but adequate for a value entry: Pfizer retains global R&D scale (~$10bn+ annually), a diversified biopharma portfolio (Prevnar, Vyndaqel, Eliquis alliance), and manufacturing infrastructure. The screen’s risk-family pass is supported by moderate leverage (D/E 72%) and investment-grade debt structure, though negative tangible equity from the $43bn Seagen acquisition limits balance-sheet optionality.

## RISKS AND RED FLAGS

**COVID cliff and revenue durability.** Filings define Comirnaty and Paxlovid explicitly; Q3 2025 product revenue fell 10% year-on-year ($13.9bn vs $15.4bn). The market debate on whether ~$63bn is a floor or still normalising lower is unresolved.

**Seagen integration and intangibles.** The December 2023 acquisition inflated goodwill/intangibles to ~$125bn. Amortisation of ~$1.2bn per quarter weighs on reported earnings. Morningstar (4 February 2026) flagged a Seagen impairment as incrementally negative. Negative tangible equity limits financial flexibility.

**Dividend coverage.** Cash dividends (~$9.8bn/year) exceed reported net income ($7.8bn FY2025) and approximate FCF ($9.1bn). Sustainable only if normalised earnings and cost savings materialise; a cut would break the dividend-screen thesis.

**Restructuring execution.** The Q1 2026 10-Q discloses the “Realigning Our Cost Base” programme: ~$4.7bn total costs through 2027, including severance, digital enablement, and non-cash asset write-downs ($3.1bn Biopharma). Failure to deliver savings would leave Pfizer structurally over-earning relative to revenue.

**Regulatory and pricing.** IRA, Medicare negotiation, and the September 2025 Trump-administration drug-pricing agreement ($70bn US investment pledge, per Economic Times coverage) create long-tail pricing pressure not captured by static screens.

**Patent and litigation.** Filing extracts reference Moderna patent-infringement cases and multi-district litigation. News (15 July 2026, TechStock²) flagged a new patent claim following ~$96bn cumulative COVID vaccine revenue. Contingencies are noted on balance sheets (“Commitments and Contingencies”) but not quantified in available body extracts.

**Pension obligations.** Defined-benefit pension/postretirement obligations of ~$2.0bn (Mar 2026)—manageable but present.

**Competitive pipeline risk.** HSBC downgraded Pfizer (6 July 2026) citing lack of near-term catalysts; obesity competition (Metsera acquisition) faces tolerability and execution uncertainty.

**Governance/credit.** No going-concern or covenant breach language in available filings. Debt is long-dated (notes due 2027–2065 referenced in XBRL). Defaults upon senior securities: N/A in all interim filings.
