# PayPal Holdings, Inc. (PYPL) — Research memo

_Version 1 · Updated 2026-07-17T04:04:58.249247+00:00 · Mode: initial_

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
PayPal is a global two-sided payments network processing $1.79 trillion of total payment volume (TPV) across 439 million active accounts, yet the shares trade on a value screen at roughly 10.7× earnings and 2.5× book with 25% return on equity and meaningful free cash flow. The quantitative buy signal rests on cheapness, quality, dividend initiation, GARP, and financial-health metrics passing together, but operating momentum is mixed: TPV is growing while transaction counts fell 4% in FY2025, and Q1 2026 revenue rose 7% year-on-year whilst net income declined. The central debate is whether capital return, cost restructuring, and platform partnerships can offset share loss to Apple Pay, Google Pay, Stripe, and BNPL rivals—or whether reported takeover interest from Stripe/Advent simply crystallises a floor rather than a re-rating catalyst. Deep research broadly supports the screen’s value case but flags medium execution and competitive risk that screens underweight.

## INVESTMENT THESIS
For a value investor, PYPL offers a rare combination of scale, profitability, and balance-sheet optionality at a depressed multiple. The screen rates **buy** with a 60% composite score (63% sector-relative), 13 of 22 models passing across five factor families—cheapness, quality, dividend, GARP, and risk—with high data quality (19/20 metrics). Headline metrics align with classic value criteria: P/E 10.7, P/B 2.54, dividend yield 1.0%, ROE 25.1%, debt/equity 58%, and positive free cash flow (~$4.1bn per screen; see financial review). Passed models include Graham Enterprising, Earnings Yield, FCF Yield, Buffett Quality, Magic Formula, Piotroski F-Score (13), and Financial Health, suggesting the market is pricing in structural decline rather than a broken franchise.

Business quality remains intact at the platform level. FY2025 10-K KPIs (primary filing) show TPV of **$1.79 trillion (+7% YoY)**, 25.4 billion payment transactions (−4%), and 439 million active accounts (+1%). PayPal’s two-sided network, trusted brands (PayPal, Venmo, Braintree), regulatory licences, and open-ecosystem positioning are cited in the 10-K as durable competitive strengths. Management has paired modest top-line growth with aggressive capital return: per Yahoo fallback data, FY2025 net income was **$5.23bn** on **$33.17bn revenue**, with **$5.56bn free cash flow**, **$6.05bn share repurchases**, and initiation of a **$130m dividend**. The stock therefore screens as a cash-generative, buyback-supported value compounder rather than a pure growth name—exactly where multi-factor value models tend to find mispricing after a multi-year de-rating.

The gap between screen conviction (44%, building) and model breadth (13 passes) is the opportunity: quantitative signals say the price is wrong; qualitative work must confirm that earnings power is sustainable.

FCF remains robust but dipped from FY2024’s peak, consistent with higher transaction expense and working-capital movements. ROE of 25.1% (screen) is supported by strong earnings on a stable equity base.

The divergence between TPV growth and transaction decline in FY2025 implies higher average payment size and/or mix shift—positive for revenue but potentially signalling lower-frequency consumer engagement.

## RISKS AND RED FLAGS
**Competitive / structural:** The 10-K describes intense competition from wallet providers (Apple Pay, Google Pay), merchant acquirers, BNPL specialists, and banks. FY2025 transaction counts fell 4% despite TPV growth—engagement metrics are softening. Rothschild Redburn downgraded to Sell (Feb 2026) citing erosion in “agentic commerce,” reflecting bearish sell-side sentiment not captured by value screens.

**Regulatory:** PayPal operates under payments and consumer-credit licences globally; the 10-K highlights regulatory complexity as both moat and burden. News reports an **FCA probe into UK wallet economics** (May 2026), which could pressure interchange/wallet economics in a key market. A reported **Stripe–Advent $53bn bid** (Jul 2026) would face significant antitrust and financing scrutiny; the board reportedly views a **$60.50/share** offer as inadequate.

**Credit / BNPL:** Nine-month transaction and credit losses rose 32% YoY in FY2025 interim filings. Installment and consumer credit portfolios are growing (loans receivable ~$6.7bn on Q1 2026 balance sheet). No going-concern language appears in available extracts; contingent liabilities are referenced (Note 13 commitments and contingencies in Q1 2026 10-Q) but detailed amounts are not in truncated bodies.

**Governance / execution:** **CEO transition**—Alex Chriss replaced by Enrique Lores (ex-HP)—followed a weak Q4/FY2025 report and ~17% single-day share drop (Feb 2026 news). President Frank Keller sold ~$196k of stock (Jun 2026). Leadership turnover adds execution risk to the turnaround narrative.

**Capital structure:** Long-term debt ~$9.4–10.0bn is manageable relative to equity and FCF, but PayPal carries large **customer funds payables** (~$41.5bn current liabilities)—inherent to the model, not traditional leverage, yet liquidity management is critical. Massive buybacks ($6bn+ annually) support EPS but reduce balance-sheet flexibility if competition intensifies.

**Pension:** No material pension obligations surfaced in available filing extracts.

**Screen blind spots:** Value metrics do not penalise declining transaction frequency, rising credit losses, or strategic uncertainty from pending M&A.
