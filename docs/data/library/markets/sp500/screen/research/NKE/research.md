# NIKE, Inc. (NKE) — Research memo

_Version 1 · Updated 2026-07-17T04:21:05.211292+00:00 · Mode: initial_

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
NIKE remains the global leader in athletic footwear and apparel, but the business is in a multi-year earnings reset: revenues have stabilised near $46bn after a sharp FY2024 peak, while net income has more than halved. The quantitative screen flags a buy on dividend yield (3.8%), depressed P/E (21.2x), and financial-health metrics, with a composite score of 63% versus 60% for the sector. The central debate is whether a cyclical trough in margins, channel mix, and China—combined with management’s pivot away from digital-first distribution—can restore growth without sacrificing the premium brand. Valuation looks optically cheap on earnings and yield, but cash conversion has weakened materially and the screen’s extreme PEG (0.05) reflects base-effect earnings recovery rather than durable growth.

## INVESTMENT THESIS
For a value investor, NKE offers a rare combination of global brand moat, investment-grade balance sheet, and an elevated dividend yield at a multi-year share-price low. The screen passes five of 22 models across three families—dividend, GARP, and risk—with Lynch PEG, Neff PEGY, High Dividend Yield, Dividend Growth, and Financial Health all clearing thresholds. Key metrics: P/E 21.2, P/B 4.4, yield 3.8%, ROE 22.1%. ROE remains respectable despite the earnings compression, and the dividend family signals suggest the market is pricing in distress that filings do not fully corroborate on solvency.

Business quality remains high per the FY2025 and FY2026 10-K business descriptions: NIKE is the largest seller of athletic footwear and apparel globally, with no single customer above 10% of consolidated revenues, diversified manufacturing (Vietnam ~52%, Indonesia ~27%, China ~16% of footwear), and ~56% of revenues from outside the US. The screen’s buy signal is best interpreted as a **valuation-and-yield entry into a quality franchise undergoing turnaround**, not a classic earnings-growth GARP story—the reported 428% “growth” driving the ultra-low PEG is a trough-recovery artefact, not a sustainable run-rate.

Share repurchases slowed sharply to $146m (vs $2.79bn prior-year period); dividends paid $1.80bn.

Earlier interim filings corroborate the pattern: Q1 FY2026 revenue $11.72bn (+1.1%) but net income $727m (−31%); Q2 FY2026 revenue $12.43bn (+0.6%) but net income $792m (−32%). Nine-month revenue is essentially flat while profitability continues to compress—consistent with elevated demand-creation spend and gross-margin pressure.

**Trend assessment:** Revenue has stabilised after the FY2024 reset, but **margin recovery is incomplete** and **cash conversion is the weakest link**. Inventories appear controlled, yet receivables growth and tariff-related working-capital dynamics (noted in recent news) warrant monitoring. No going-concern language appears in available filing extracts.

**Gaps:** Ten 8-K filings are indexed without body extracts; annual 10-K body extracts lack audited income-statement tables. Full-year FY2026 audited figures should be cross-checked against the 15 Jul 2026 10-K when complete body text becomes available.

## RISKS AND RED FLAGS
**Trade and tariffs.** The FY2025 and FY2026 10-K business sections state that “protectionist measures have resulted in increases in the cost of our products, and additional measures, if implemented, will adversely affect sales and/or profitability… possibly materially.” U.S. tariff policy and retaliatory measures “have negatively affected, and could in the future materially negatively affect” NIKE. Recent news (“Nike Tariff Receivables Put Cash Flow in Focus,” 16 Jul 2026) aligns with the receivables build visible in Q3 FY2026 filings.

**China and competition.** Greater China remains a structural weak point; news coverage cites weakness in China alongside margin pressure (e.g. “Nike Has a China and Margin Problem,” 1 Apr 2026). Filings name adidas, Anta, On, lululemon, and others as intense global competitors, with failure to respond to consumer preference shifts flagged as a “continuing risk.”

**Strategy reversal.** Multiple reports indicate NIKE is retreating from its digital-first, direct-to-consumer emphasis as Nike Digital and Direct sales decline (“Nike (NKE) Rethinks Digital First Growth,” 14 Jul 2026)—raising execution risk on the prior multi-year strategic pillar.

**Governance and tax.** Q3 FY2026 notes an ongoing IRS audit for fiscal years 2017–2023, with $969m gross unrecognized tax benefits at 28 Feb 2026. A European Commission investigation into Netherlands tax rulings remains open; an adverse outcome could increase prior-period tax liabilities. These are contingencies, not quantified liabilities, but they add tail risk.

**Restructuring.** Employee severance is referenced in FY2024–FY2026 filing metadata and news of a second layoff round (April 2026). Restructuring costs support margin normalisation but signal organisational strain.

**Balance-sheet signals.** Retained earnings have turned negative (deficit $610m at Feb 2026 per 10-Q), reflecting sustained dividend and buyback distributions through an earnings trough. This is not a solvency issue given $6.7bn cash and modest net debt, but it limits capital-allocation flexibility if earnings do not recover.

**Pension:** No material pension underfunding is highlighted in available extracts; pension-related current liabilities are present on the balance sheet but not flagged as a primary risk factor in the indexed bodies.

No going-concern qualification appears in available filing language.
