# Copart, Inc. (CPRT) — Research memo

_Version 1 · Updated 2026-07-17T03:19:05.161857+00:00 · Mode: initial_

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Copart operates the leading global online salvage-vehicle auction platform, earning high-margin fees on total-loss and recovered vehicles sourced overwhelmingly from insurers. The quantitative screen rates CPRT a **strong buy** (7/22 models, 83% composite): cheapness (P/E 17.5, P/B ~3.0), quality (ROE 17.6%), and balance-sheet strength (D/E ~1%) align with a wide-moat compounder trading well below recent highs. FY2025 revenue reached $4.6 billion with $1.7 billion operating income (FY2025 10-K MD&A), yet the share price has fallen ~33–40% over the past year amid U.S. insurance-assignment volume softness. The central debate is whether international growth, revenue-per-car expansion, and aggressive buybacks offset cyclical U.S. headwinds—and whether Jay Adair’s return as CEO from 31 July 2026 re-accelerates growth and M&A.

## INVESTMENT THESIS
Copart fits a value investor’s checklist on fundamentals even as the market prices in near-term volume weakness. The screen passes Graham Enterprising, Quality Value, Buffett Quality, Economic Moat, Magic Formula, Composite Value, and Financial Health—three of four factor families (cheapness, quality, risk) with 18/20 data quality. At P/E 17.5 and P/B ~3.0 against mid-teens ROE and ~$1.0 billion annual free cash flow (Yahoo fallback: $1.23 billion FCF in FY2025), the market assigns a discount to a business that has compounded revenue from $3.9 billion (FY2023, Yahoo) to $4.6 billion (FY2025, 10-K MD&A) while maintaining operating margins near 37%.

Business quality rests on durable competitive advantages: proprietary VB3 auction technology, a dense global yard network, scale in advance-charge logistics, and catastrophe-response capability. Insurance companies supplied 81%, 81%, and 83% of vehicles processed in FY2025, 2024, and 2023 respectively (FY2025 10-K), creating concentration risk but also deep switching costs. Growth levers cited in filings—higher gross selling prices, value-added services, non-insurance sellers, and international yard expansion—remain intact even as U.S. unit volumes soften. Management repurchased ~43.4 million shares for ~$1.43 billion in Q3 FY2026 alone (Q3 FY2026 10-Q), signalling confidence at depressed prices. For a value investor, the screen’s strong-buy signal is supported by a net-cash balance sheet, minimal leverage, and a franchise earning power that has not materially deteriorated despite the share-price drawdown.

## RISKS AND RED FLAGS
**Insurance concentration and U.S. volume cyclicality.** Insurers provide 81–83% of vehicle supply (FY2025 10-K). Q1–Q2 FY2026 MD&A explicitly cites U.S. volume declines, partially offset by higher revenue per car and international growth. Lower accident frequency, improved vehicle repair economics, or insurer cost-cutting could reduce salvage assignments—a headwind not fully captured by backward-looking screen metrics.

**Seasonality and catastrophe exposure.** Filings note winter volumes run 5–20% above average and that hurricanes and severe weather can spike assignment volumes (operational opportunity but also logistics strain). Copart mobilised resources for Hurricane Helene flood vehicles (FY2025 10-K business description).

**Competition and regulatory complexity.** The 10-K references competition and extensive DMV/title regulatory requirements across jurisdictions. Ordinary-course litigation and regulatory inquiries are disclosed; Note 9 (Q3 FY2026 10-Q) states there are no material pending legal proceedings beyond routine injury, property, and contract claims.

**Governance and leadership transition.** CEO Jeff Liaw steps down on 31 July 2026; Executive Chairman Jay Adair returns as CEO, with Jane Pocock becoming President from 1 August 2026. A “reverse transition” introduces execution uncertainty around strategy, M&A, and AI initiatives signalled in recent news—though Adair is a long-tenured founder-figure.

**Insider selling.** Director Daniel Englander sold ~$2.2 million of shares (15 July 2026, news manifest); prior CEO option exercises and sales also reported. Not necessarily bearish but worth monitoring alongside large buybacks.

**Advance charges and working capital.** Copart routinely pays advance charges on behalf of sellers to release vehicles from tow/repair facilities (FY2025 10-K). Scale supports this, but it ties up working capital and creates recovery risk in stress scenarios.

**Cybersecurity.** Item 1C is referenced in the 10-K index; full cybersecurity disclosure is not present in truncated body extracts.

**Market vs fundamentals disconnect.** The screen’s cheapness metrics partly reflect a ~33–40% share-price decline (news, July 2026) despite flat nine-month earnings—a potential value trap signal if U.S. volumes deteriorate further.

No going-concern, covenant breach, or material contingent liability language appears in available filing extracts. Pension risk is not flagged.
