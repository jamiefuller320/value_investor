# QUALCOMM Incorporated (QCOM) — Research memo

_Version 1 · Updated 2026-07-16T19:27:41.191162+00:00 · Mode: initial_

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Qualcomm remains a high-quality compounder trading at a value-oriented multiple: the screen flags Strong Buy on cheapness, quality, dividend, GARP and risk metrics (P/E 18.3, FCF yield 5.3%, ROE 36.1%, dividend yield 2.1%). The investment case rests on a durable patent-licensing franchise (QTL) funding a diversified semiconductor platform (QCT) expanding into automotive, IoT and data centre AI, with aggressive capital return ($20bn buyback announced March 2026). The central debate is whether diversification and cash generation offset structural handset share loss as Apple insources modems, cyclical memory-driven OEM destocking, and persistent regulatory pressure on licensing. Deep research broadly confirms the screen’s quality-and-value pairing, but moderates conviction on near-term earnings visibility.

## INVESTMENT THESIS
The quantitative screen’s Strong Buy rating is well grounded in business quality, not just statistical cheapness. Qualcomm passes ten of twenty-two models—including FCF Yield, Lynch PEG (0.11), Neff PEGY, Quality Value, Buffett Quality, Economic Moat, Dividend Growth, Magic Formula, Composite Value and Financial Health—across five factor families with 19/20 data completeness. That breadth is unusual for a large-cap semiconductor name and reflects a business model that blends asset-light, high-margin licensing (QTL EBT margins ~72–75% per interim filings) with scale semiconductor design (QCT).

For a value investor, the hook is twofold. First, the market appears to price cyclical handset weakness and Apple modem displacement into the multiple, whilst underweighting the annuity-like QTL cash stream and the structural growth in automotive (+26% H1 FY2026 YoY to $2.4bn) and IoT (+9% to $3.4bn). Second, management is returning capital at a pace that supports total return even if the share price stagnates—the FY2025 10-K notes cumulative repurchases and dividends, and the Q2 FY2026 10-Q records a new $20.0bn authorisation (total authorised $21.9bn remaining at 29 March 2026) plus a dividend increase to $0.92 per quarter.

Business quality indicators align with screen outputs: gross margins remain above 54%, R&D exceeds $9bn annually (FY2025, Yahoo fallback), and free cash flow conversion is strong. The Alphawave acquisition ($2.3bn, December 2025) and six smaller FY2026 deals signal a credible diversification into data-centre connectivity—an area where the screen’s elevated growth metric (173%) may partly reflect normalised earnings rebound rather than sustainable top-line acceleration, warranting caution on headline growth.

Handset weakness—management cites OEM inventory adjustments due to memory supply constraints—is partially offset by automotive and IoT mix shift. Operating margins are compressing modestly (gross margin −1pp; R&D and SG&A rising on diversification investments and Alphawave integration). ## RISKS AND RED FLAGS
**Regulatory and litigation.** QTL’s licensing model faces ongoing global scrutiny. The FY2025 10-K warns that “changes in our patent licensing practices, whether due to governmental investigations, legal challenges or otherwise, could adversely impact our business.” The Q2 FY2026 10-Q details active proceedings: ParkerVision patent damages (Federal Circuit appeal pending); Arm Ltd. dispute over Nuvia/Oryon CPU licensing (jury verdict largely in Qualcomm’s favour; Arm appealing); and numerous other IP actions. Contingent losses are not accrued as of 29 March 2026; the filing states outcomes are “inherently uncertain” and unfavourable resolution “could have a material adverse effect.” A UK smartphone-chip antitrust suit was reported withdrawn (February 2026, news manifest), but regulatory overhang persists globally.

**Customer concentration and vertical integration.** Apple, Samsung and Xiaomi each exceeded 10% of FY2025 revenue (10-K). The Q2 FY2026 MD&A explicitly warns that Apple “will increasingly use its own modem products, rather than our products, in its future devices, which will have a significant negative impact on our QCT revenues, results of operations and cash flows.” This is the single largest structural risk not fully captured by backward-looking screen metrics.

**Cyclical and macro.** The 10-Q cites the “highly cyclical semiconductor industry” and current memory supply constraints adversely affecting handset OEM demand. Trade policy uncertainty (U.S./China tariffs, export controls) and China revenue concentration are flagged as material. Inventory rose to $7.4bn at 29 March 2026 from $6.5bn at FY2025 year-end, with $782m in multi-year capacity prepayments—signalling forward supply commitments amid demand uncertainty.

**Tax volatility.** FY2025–FY2026 earnings are distorted by CAMT/OBBB interactions: a $5.7bn valuation allowance in FY2025 followed by a $5.7bn release in Q2 FY2026. Unrecognised tax benefits stood at $2.9bn (10-Q). This complicates P/E-based screening.

**Governance.** No going-concern language appears in indexed filings. CEO Cristiano Amon sold $1.85m in shares (May 2026, news manifest)—immaterial relative to holdings but worth monitoring alongside heavy buybacks.

**Pension.** No material pension red flags surfaced in available filing extracts.
