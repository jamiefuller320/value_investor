# Uber Technologies, Inc. (UBER) — Research memo

_Version 1 · Updated 2026-07-17T04:20:23.825411+00:00 · Mode: initial_

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Uber has transitioned from a loss-making disruptor to a cash-generative global mobility and delivery platform, with FY2025 revenue of $52.0bn (Yahoo fallback) and operating income more than doubling year-on-year. The quantitative screen rates the name a buy on cheapness, quality, and risk metrics (P/E 18.2, ROE 35.3%, profit margin 15.9%, debt/equity 48%), passing five models including Quality Value, Buffett Quality, and Financial Health. The valuation hook is mid-teens earnings against accelerating free cash flow (~$9.8bn in FY2025 per Yahoo) and a strengthening balance sheet (cash $7.1bn, net debt ~$3.4bn at Dec 2025). The central debate is whether network-scale economics and the pending $14.8bn Delivery Hero acquisition justify the multiple, or whether regulatory M&A risk, worker-classification litigation, autonomous-vehicle disruption, and volatile investment gains will cap re-rating—evidenced by a 52-week low in June 2026 and a weak Q1 2026 GAAP print despite strong operating income.

## INVESTMENT THESIS
The screen's buy signal aligns with a business that now exhibits classic quality-value characteristics rather than speculative growth. Uber passes cheapness, quality, and risk families (composite 66%, sector-relative 67%, 18/20 data quality), with highlights including ROE ≥12% (35.3%), profit margin ≥5% (15.9%), and debt/equity below 80% (48%). For a value investor, the case rests on three pillars.

First, operating leverage and cash conversion. Revenue grew from $37.3bn (FY2023) to $52.0bn (FY2025) per Yahoo fallback, while operating income rose from $1.1bn to $5.6bn. FY2025 free cash flow reached $9.8bn (Yahoo), against capex of just $336m. The company repurchased $6.5bn of stock in FY2025 (Yahoo).

Second, platform moat and segment economics. Q1 2025 interim filings (10-Q) show segment Adjusted EBITDA of $2,509m: Mobility $1,753m, Delivery $763m, Freight approximately breakeven. Cross-platform membership (Uber One) and multi-product usage—cited in the FY2024 10-K body—reinforce retention.

Third, balance-sheet optionality. Filing-sourced balance sheets show total assets rising from $51.2bn (Dec 2024) to $61.8bn (Dec 2025), stockholders' equity from $21.6bn to $27.0bn, and long-term debt at $10.5bn (Q1 2026 10-Q, Dec 2025 column).

The screen's low timing conviction (27%, building, two weeks at signal) suggests the buy is fundamental rather than tactical. Value investors should treat Uber as a profitable platform compounder at a reasonable multiple, not a deep-value asset play—P/B of 6.0 reflects intangible network value and deferred tax assets ($11.0bn at Dec 2025 per Q1 2026 10-Q).

## RISKS AND RED FLAGS
Worker classification and litigation: FY2025 10-K and Q1 2026 10-Q forward-looking statements prioritise defending litigation on driver and courier relationships. UK VAT exposure flagged in Jan 2026 news.

Regulatory and M&A: the $14.8bn Delivery Hero deal (Jul 2026) requires multi-jurisdiction antitrust approval. Goodwill rose to $8.9bn at Dec 2025. Filings reference commitments and contingencies (Notes 9–11) without full quantification in extracts.

Autonomous vehicles: FY2025 10-K names Waymo, Zoox, and Tesla as competitors. Partnership activity (Baidu, Rivian) is strategic but long-term disintermediation risk remains.

Competition: DoorDash, Instacart, Delivery Hero, Grab, and incentive-driven rivals flagged in filings.

Earnings quality: Q1 2026 showed $1.5bn securities losses through other income; retained earnings remain negative ($(10,628)m at Dec 2025). Reported ROE may be flattered by deferred tax assets and episodic gains.

Insurance reserves exceeded $12.9bn combined at Dec 2025—a material liability structure. No going-concern language in available extracts.
