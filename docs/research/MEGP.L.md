# ME Group International plc (MEGP.L) — Research memo

_Version 1 · Updated 2026-07-14T12:02:59.437944+00:00 · Mode: initial_

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
ME Group International operates unattended photobooth and laundry networks globally; the quantitative screen rates it a strong buy on cheapness (P/E 7.5, yield 7.4%), quality (ROE 28.2%), dividend, GARP, and risk metrics, with 14 of 22 models passing and a composite score of 78%. Reported revenue has grown steadily from £259.8m (FY2022) to £315.4m (FY2025), with operating margins expanding to 24.5%, whilst net debt has fallen materially and the group remains in a net cash position. The central debate is whether recent French consumer weakness and photobooth softness are transient trading blips — as management now suggests — or evidence of cyclical vulnerability in a capex-heavy, dividend-led model where free cash flow has declined despite rising earnings. Valuation appears compelling on screen metrics, but conviction is tempered by a fresh signal (one week), neutral technical timing, and a June profit-guidance cut followed by a July rebound narrative.

## INVESTMENT THESIS
The screen’s strong-buy signal rests on a rare combination of deep value and operational quality. At P/E 7.5, P/B 2.0, and a 7.4% dividend yield, the stock passes classic enterprising-value filters (Graham Enterprising, Earnings Yield, Low P/E + High Yield, Neff PEGY, Acquirer’s Multiple, Dreman Contrarian) alongside quality screens (Buffett Quality, Economic Moat, Magic Formula, Financial Health). Debt/equity of 28% aligns with a deleveraging balance sheet: total debt fell from £118.1m (FY2022) to £43.0m (FY2025), whilst shareholders’ equity rose from £132.6m to £213.0m over the same period.

Business quality corroborates the screen. Normalised EBITDA grew from £87.8m to £120.6m (FY2022–FY2025), diluted EPS rose from 10.23p to 14.91p, and operating cash flow has been consistently strong at £78.7m–£90.8m annually. The installed-base model — evidenced by net PPE rising from £101.1m to £169.5m — supports recurring, high-margin revenue from coin-operated and card-payment assets. Dividend screens pass (High Dividend Yield, Dividend Growth), and cash dividends of £23.4m–£35.5m annually indicate a shareholder-return orientation consistent with the yield signal.

For a value investor, the hook is a profitable, asset-backed franchise trading at a single-digit earnings multiple with net cash, rising returns on equity, and broad model confirmation across five factor families — yet still priced below its 200-day moving average with neutral RSI (48), suggesting the market has not fully re-rated the recovery.

## FINANCIAL REVIEW
Note: FY2021 income-statement, balance-sheet, and cash-flow data in the source file are largely absent; the analysis below covers FY2022–FY2025 (four complete years).

Revenue and margins. Total revenue grew from £259.8m (FY2022) to £315.4m (FY2025), a compound trajectory of roughly 6–7% per annum, moderating from +14.6% (2022–2023) to +2.4% (2024–2025). Gross profit rose from £81.4m to £113.0m; gross margin expanded from 31.3% to 35.8%. Operating income increased from £49.4m to £77.2m, with the operating margin improving from 19.0% to 24.5%. EBITDA grew from £94.3m to £122.7m. Net income rose from £38.8m to £56.6m; diluted EPS increased from 10.23p to 14.91p.

Leverage and balance-sheet strength. Total debt declined sharply from £118.1m (FY2022) to £43.0m (FY2025), with long-term debt falling from £72.4m to £12.4m. Cash and equivalents fell from £135.2m to £56.5m over the period, but the group retained a net cash position throughout (approximately £13–17m net cash in each year). Shareholders’ equity grew 61% to £213.0m; tangible book value reached £185.6m. Working capital was positive at £46.9m (FY2025). Non-current pension obligations are modest at £4.6m. Capital lease obligations totalled £13.0m. Inventory has risen materially — from £25.5m to £47.7m — which bears monitoring for working-capital drag.

Cash generation. Operating cash flow was robust and stable: £78.7m (FY2022), £87.7m (FY2023), £85.9m (FY2024), and £90.8m (FY2025). Capital expenditure accelerated from £38.9m to £65.6m, reflecting network expansion and asset refresh. Consequently, free cash flow declined from £39.7m (FY2022) to £25.2m (FY2025), a trend the screen’s FCF metric may underweight relative to earnings-based models. The group returned £29.8m in dividends (FY2025) and continued debt repayments (£21.5m in FY2025), funded by operating cash rather than incremental borrowing.

Overall, the five-year financial picture (four complete years) shows a company growing revenue and margins, deleveraging, maintaining net cash, and investing heavily in its asset base — supportive of quality and risk screen passes, but with a widening gap between reported earnings growth and declining free cash flow.

## RISKS AND RED FLAGS
Cyclical and geographic exposure. News flow in June 2026 highlighted a French consumer slowdown that triggered a profit-guidance reduction and a 22–26% share-price fall; although July 2026 updates described a rebound, this underscores sensitivity to discretionary spending in key European markets. Photobooth trading was specifically cited as soft in H1 2026, partially offset by laundry growth — segment mix risk is not fully captured by aggregate financial ratios.

Capex and FCF intensity. Rising capex (£65.6m in FY2025) and inventory build have compressed FCF to £25.2m despite £90.8m of operating cash flow. A value screen anchored on earnings yield may overstate distributable capacity if investment requirements remain elevated.

Governance. Investors’ Chronicle (4 June 2026) flagged “boardroom problems” linked to the share-price tumble — a governance concern that quantitative risk models typically cannot assess.

Dividend sustainability. Dividend payments (£29.8m in FY2025) exceed free cash flow (£25.2m), implying dividends are partly funded by balance-sheet cash (which fell £21.0m in FY2025). The 7.4% screen yield is attractive but warrants scrutiny if FCF does not recover.

Pension and leases. Pension liabilities (£4.6m) are small relative to equity, but capitalised leases (£13.0m) add to fixed obligations. FX volatility is evident in cash-flow statements (e.g. £3.3m exchange effect in FY2025).

Competitive and regulatory. Unattended retail faces site-access negotiations, data-privacy regulation (photobooth imaging), and competition from digital photography and alternative laundry formats — none of which appear in the screen.

Signal stability. The strong-buy rating is new (one week, conviction 49%), so the quantitative signal lacks track record on this name.
