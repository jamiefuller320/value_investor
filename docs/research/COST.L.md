The quantitative screen passes Graham Enterprising, FCF Yield, Lynch PEG, Neff PEGY, Quality Value, Buffett Quality, Dividend Growth, Magic Formula, Acquirer's Multiple, Composite Value, and Financial Health — spanning all five metric families (cheapness, quality, dividend, GARP, risk). Business quality is improving on a per-pound-of-revenue basis: gross profit rose to £113.8m on revenue of £1,045.7m in FY2025 (implied gross margin ~10.9%) versus £92.7m on £1,421.4m in FY2022 (~6.5%). Operating income reached £44.8m (FY2025) from £26.8m (FY2023), and diluted EPS climbed to 13.7p from 7.8p over the same period. Balance-sheet discipline supports the quality screen: total debt is only £25.0m (entirely capital lease obligations), stockholders' equity has grown to £258.2m from £211.2m (FY2022–FY2025), and cash and equivalents stand at £189.3m. The screen's low leverage (debt/equity 10%) and dividend-family pass are corroborated by £7.3m dividends paid and £12.0m share repurchases in FY2025. Conviction on the screen itself is modest at 40% (new signal, one week at buy), and timing is neutral — consistent with accumulating on weakness rather than chasing momentum.

## FINANCIAL REVIEW
**Revenue.** Reported revenue has contracted materially over four complete fiscal years: £1,421.4m (2022) → £1,332.0m (2023) → £1,251.1m (2024) → £1,045.7m (2025), a decline of roughly 26%. FY2021 income-statement data is largely absent from the source file (only rent expense supplemental of £41.3m is populated), so a full five-year revenue series cannot be constructed.

**Margins.** Despite lower revenue, profitability metrics have strengthened. Gross profit increased from £92.7m (2022) to £113.8m (2025). Operating income rose from £31.1m (2024) to £44.8m (2025), and from £26.8m (2023). EBITDA grew steadily: £48.6m (2022) → £50.9m (2023) → £52.6m (2024) → £65.3m (2025). Net income followed: £25.9m (2022) → £22.1m (2023) → £30.6m (2024) → £37.3m (2025), with diluted EPS of 9.4p, 7.8p, 11.1p, and 13.7p respectively. SG&A has been controlled at £69.0m (2025) versus £78.0m (2023).

**Leverage.** Financial leverage is low. Total debt was £29.5m (2022), £24.3m (2023), £25.8m (2024), and £25.0m (2025), with no material long-term bank debt disclosed in recent years — obligations are predominantly capital leases (£16.5m long-term; £8.5m current in FY2025). Invested capital of £258.2m equals equity, indicating an unlevered capital structure. Total liabilities of £303.7m against total assets of £561.9m imply a conservative funding profile.

**Cash generation.** Operating cash flow was £14.1m (2022), £69.8m (2023), £42.7m (2024), and £51.3m (2025). Free cash flow followed a similar but more volatile path: £13.6m (2022) → £69.7m (2023) → £33.6m (2024) → £48.5m (2025). FY2022 working-capital outflows of £35.9m and FY2025 outflows of £9.5m explain much of the volatility. Capital expenditure remains modest (£2.8m in FY2025). End cash position reached £215.3m (including restricted cash of £26.0m).

**Balance-sheet strength.** Cash and equivalents of £189.3m, working capital of £125.0m (up from £67.7m in 2022), and tangible book value of £207.1m provide a solid buffer. Defined pension benefit liabilities are £60.0m (FY2025), up from £53.5m (2023) — a material off-balance-sheet-style commitment. Other receivables of £137.4m and accounts receivable of £54.1m represent significant contract-billing exposure. Goodwill of £45.1m is stable. Equity has been rebuilt through retained earnings (£103.4m in FY2025) and ongoing buybacks.

## RISKS AND RED FLAGS
**Revenue contraction.** The four-year revenue decline is the most prominent financial red flag. Without fuller FY2021 data, it is unclear how much reflects deliberate contract exits versus competitive losses, but the screen's growth-oriented models may understate this risk.

**Contract and working-capital risk.** FY2022 demonstrated how working-capital swings can absorb cash (operating cash flow of only £14.1m on net income of £25.9m). Large other receivables (£137.4m) warrant scrutiny for recoverability and contract-milestone timing — not captured by static balance-sheet screens.

**Pension.** The defined benefit obligation of £60.0m (~23% of equity) is a structural liability that could re-emerge if discount rates or longevity assumptions move adversely.

**Cyclical and regulatory exposure.** As a UK infrastructure and engineering services contractor, Costain is exposed to public-sector spending cycles, political shifts in infrastructure prioritisation (transport, energy, water, defence), and cost inflation on fixed-price contracts. Screens do not model procurement delays or client concentration.

**Governance and execution.** No governance controversies appear in the news manifest, but the absence of detailed operational reporting in the feed limits visibility into order-book quality, margin backlog, or provisioning trends.

**Competitive.** UK contracting remains fragmented and price-competitive; margin gains may prove difficult to sustain if the group rebids for volume to replace lost revenue.
