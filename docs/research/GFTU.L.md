# Grafton Group plc (GFTU.L) — Research memo

_Version 1 · Updated 2026-07-14T12:05:18.269517+00:00 · Mode: initial_

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Grafton Group plc is a pan-European building materials merchant and distributor trading at a modest absolute and sector-relative valuation (P/E 12.8, P/B 1.0, 4.2% yield) on a quantitative screen that rates the name a strong buy (10/22 models; composite 84%). The investment case rests on resilient cash generation, a liquid balance sheet, and a disciplined capital-return programme (dividends plus recurring buybacks), offset by earnings and margins that remain well below the 2022 cyclical peak despite 2025 revenue reaching a new high of £2.52bn. The central debate is whether cheapness and buyback support are sufficient compensation for cyclical UK/Irish exposure, lease-adjusted leverage, and goodwill-heavy M&A history — or whether margin normalisation and the 2030 growth plan can restore mid-cycle profitability. Deep-dive financials broadly support the screen’s value framing but temper conviction given profit compression and limited qualitative news depth.

## INVESTMENT THESIS
The quantitative screen flags Grafton as a strong buy across all four value families — cheapness, dividend, GARP, and risk — with high data quality (19/20 metrics). Passed models include Graham Defensive, Graham Enterprising, Schloss Low P/B, FCF Yield, Neff PEGY, Dividend Growth, Acquirer’s Multiple, Dreman Contrarian, Composite Value, and Financial Health. Key hooks — P/E below 15, P/B near book, current ratio 2.11, and FCF yield — align with a classic value profile rather than a distressed one.

Business quality, as evidenced in the accounts, supports that framing: Grafton generates consistent free cash flow (£160m–£220m annually, 2022–2025), maintains substantial liquidity (£396m cash plus £100m short-term investments at FY2025), and returns capital systematically (FY2025 dividends £73m; buybacks £57m; prior-year buybacks up to £159m in 2023). Revenue has re-accelerated to £2.52bn in 2025 (+10.4% vs 2024), and management is signalling operational momentum into H1 2026 (per news flow). For a value investor, the screen’s cheapness/risk combination is credible: the market appears to price in permanently lower margins post-2022 without fully crediting balance-sheet strength, FCF durability, and ongoing shareholder returns.

## FINANCIAL REVIEW
Note: `financials_annual.json` provides full income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow for FY2022–FY2025 only; FY2021 contains partial fields (e.g. discontinued operations net income £134m, diluted EPS £1.43) and is excluded from trend analysis below.

Revenue: Total revenue was £2.30bn (2022), £2.32bn (2023), £2.28bn (2024), and £2.52bn (2025). After a modest dip in 2024, 2025 delivered the strongest top-line in the series (+10.4% year-on-year).

Margins: Gross profit rose from £869m (2022) to £955m (2025), but gross margin has been broadly stable at 37–38%. Operating income fell sharply from £241m (2022) to £154m (2024) before recovering to £162m (2025); operating margin compressed from 10.5% (2022) to 6.4% (2025). EBITDA followed a similar path: £387m (2022) → £333m (2023) → £312m (2024) → £340m (2025). Net income peaked at £209m (2022), troughed at £122m (2024), and recovered to £137m (2025) — still ~35% below the 2022 high. Normalised income (£131m in 2025 vs £210m in 2022) confirms the underlying earnings decline is not solely one-off noise.

Leverage: Total debt was £703m (2022), £633m (2023), £641m (2024), and £619m (2025). Reported long-term debt is modest (£191m in 2025), but capital lease obligations are material (£397m in 2025; £404m in 2024), implying meaningful off-balance-sheet-style financing of the branch/depot estate. Equity was £1.75bn (2022) and £1.65bn (2025). Pension liabilities appear contained: defined pension benefit £11.6m and non-current pension/post-retirement plans £4.0m (2025).

Cash generation: Operating cash flow was £217m (2022), £273m (2023), £247m (2024), and £252m (2025). Free cash flow was £160m, £220m, £200m, and £206m over the same period — consistently positive and broadly tracking EBITDA less capex (£46–58m annually). Ending cash was £712m (2022), £384m (2023), £351m (2024), and £396m (2025); the 2023 decline reflects heavy buybacks (£159m) and investment activity rather than operational weakness.

Balance sheet: Total assets grew from £2.99bn (2022) to £2.82bn (2025). Working capital remains strong (£876m in 2022; £645m in 2025). Goodwill and intangibles total £790m (2025), with goodwill alone at £659m — a legacy of the acquisition-led model. Inventory (£395m) and receivables (£224m) rose with revenue in 2025, warranting monitoring but not alarming relative to scale. Share count has fallen (ordinary shares 223.4m in 2022 to 191.3m in 2025), consistent with buyback activity.

## RISKS AND RED FLAGS
Cyclical exposure: Building materials distribution is inherently tied to residential and commercial construction cycles. The 2022–2024 margin and earnings compression — despite relatively flat revenue — suggests operating deleverage and/or mix pressure (UK market weakness cited in FY2025 earnings commentary in news). A renewed downturn could stall the 2025 recovery.

Lease-adjusted leverage: Headline debt looks manageable, but £397m of capital lease obligations (2025) materially increases fixed charges; interest expense was £23.9m in 2025. Screens may understate economic leverage.

Goodwill and M&A integration: £659m goodwill (2025) against tangible book of £858m implies acquisition risk. Cash flow shows ongoing bolt-on spend (e.g. purchase of business £69m in 2024, £24m in 2025) and restructuring/M&A charges across years.

Earnings quality vs. peak: ROE on the screen is 8.4% — acceptable but subdued versus historical mid-cycle levels implied by 2022 profitability. Recovery to prior earnings power is unproven in the five-year data.

Pension: Liabilities are small in absolute terms but should be watched if discount rates or asset returns move adversely.

Governance/news gaps: No material regulatory or governance events appear in the news manifest; absence of coverage is not evidence of clean governance. Institutional ownership (~48%, per yfinance headline Nov 2025) may amplify volatility on earnings misses.

Competitive: Fragmented European merchanting invites price competition; scale advantages help, but margin defence in weaker markets remains a live risk.
