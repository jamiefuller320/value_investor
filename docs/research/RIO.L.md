# Rio Tinto Group (RIO.L) — Research memo

_Version 1 · Updated 2026-07-13T16:01:55.807022+00:00 · Mode: initial_

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Rio Tinto screens as a Strong Buy on quantitative value metrics (10/22 models, composite score 74%), anchored by a 14.6x P/E, 2.4x P/B, 4.5% dividend yield, and 16.4% ROE at modest leverage (35% debt/equity). The investment case rests on a world-class iron ore franchise, a pivot towards copper and battery metals, and a shareholder-return policy that has historically been generous. The central debate is whether current earnings multiples adequately reflect a cyclical downshift in commodity margins and a step-up in capital deployment — 2025 free cash flow fell to $4.5bn on $12.3bn of capex and $6.0bn of acquisitions, while net debt rose to $15.0bn. Valuation looks attractive on earnings but less so on cash flow, and the screen’s conviction score (45%) remains low given the signal is only one week old.

## INVESTMENT THESIS
The quantitative screen identifies Rio Tinto across five factor families — cheapness, quality, dividend, GARP, and risk — passing classic value screens including Graham Enterprising, Neff PEGY, Buffett Quality, Magic Formula, and Acquirer’s Multiple. At 14.6x earnings and 2.4x book, the shares trade below typical large-cap mining multiples despite a 16.4% ROE and a balance sheet that, until recently, carried net debt below $6bn. Business quality is underpinned by low-cost Pilbara iron ore operations, growing copper exposure (Oyu Tolgoi, Resolution), and a lithium footprint via Rincon and Arcadium-related assets. For a value investor, the hook is a high-quality cyclical at a mid-cycle earnings multiple with a well-covered 4.5% yield. The screen’s Financial Health pass (D/E 35%) aligns with five-year equity growth from $50.6bn (2022) to $62.2bn (2025), though rising capex and M&A spending in 2025 suggest the quality dividend may face near-term pressure if commodity prices soften further.

## FINANCIAL REVIEW
**Revenue.** Total revenue was $55.5bn (2022), $54.0bn (2023), $53.7bn (2024), and rebounded to $57.6bn (2025). The 2022–2024 period showed modest contraction; 2025’s 7.4% year-on-year increase marks a recovery, though 2021 income-statement detail is unavailable in the source file.

**Margins.** Gross profit fell from $34.3bn (2022) to $30.3bn (2024) before recovering to $33.1bn (2025), implying gross margins compressed from roughly 62% to 56% and stabilised near 57%. Operating income declined from $19.4bn (2022) to $14.8bn (2024), edging up to $15.4bn (2025); operating margins moved from ~35% towards ~27%. EBITDA was $25.5bn (2022), $21.1bn (2023), $23.2bn (2024), and $23.0bn (2025) — still well below the 2022 peak. Net income and diluted EPS followed a similar arc: net income of $12.4bn / EPS $7.62 (2022), $10.1bn / $6.17 (2023), $11.6bn / $7.07 (2024), and $10.0bn / $6.08 (2025).

**Leverage.** Total debt rose from $12.3bn (2022) to $23.5bn (2025), driven by $16.0bn of long-term debt issuance against $8.2bn of repayments in 2025. Net debt jumped from $5.3bn (2022) and $5.6bn (2024) to $15.0bn (2025), while cash and equivalents held roughly flat at $6.9bn. Stockholders’ equity grew steadily from $50.6bn to $62.2bn, and total assets expanded to $128.1bn (2025) from $96.8bn (2022), reflecting both organic investment and acquisitions.

**Cash generation.** Free cash flow has trended down materially: $9.4bn (2022), $8.1bn (2023), $6.0bn (2024), and $4.5bn (2025). Operating cash flow was $16.1bn (2022), $15.2bn (2023), and $15.6bn (2024); the 2025 operating cash flow figure is not separately reported in the source file. Capital expenditure accelerated from $6.8bn (2022) to $12.3bn (2025), and 2025 included $6.0bn of business purchases. Dividends paid were $11.7bn (2022, an unusually large payout year), $6.5bn (2023), $7.0bn (2024), and $6.1bn (2025).

**Balance-sheet strength.** Tangible book value rose from $47.5bn (2022) to $56.8bn (2025). Working capital remained positive at $6.6bn (2025). Pension and post-retirement obligations total roughly $2.7bn (non-current $1.1bn plus current $1.6bn in 2025). Long-term provisions of $17.1bn (chiefly rehabilitation and closure liabilities) represent a significant off-balance-sheet-style commitment typical of the sector. Overall, the balance sheet remains investment-grade in character, but the 2025 leverage step-up warrants monitoring.

## RISKS AND RED FLAGS
**Cyclical exposure.** Iron ore and copper prices drive the majority of earnings; the 2022–2024 margin compression visible in the financials illustrates sensitivity to commodity downturns. A further price decline could pressure the dividend despite the current yield screen pass.

**Capital intensity and M&A.** The 2025 surge in capex ($12.3bn) and acquisitions ($6.0bn), funded partly by $16.0bn of new debt issuance, raises execution and returns-on-capital risk. Free cash flow at $4.5bn may not fully support both growth investment and $6.1bn of dividends without additional borrowing or asset sales.

**Governance and reputational history.** Rio Tinto’s track record includes the 2020 Juukan Gorge destruction (not covered in the news manifest but a known governance overhang) and ongoing scrutiny of its relationships with indigenous communities and regulators in Australia, Mongolia, and the US.

**Pension and provisions.** Combined pension obligations (~$2.7bn) and long-term provisions ($17.1bn) create material long-dated liabilities that screens based on debt/equity may understate.

**Growth pipeline uncertainty.** UBS (July 2026) flagged a potential copper growth gap post-2030, noting Rio may need to accelerate projects such as Argentina’s Los Azules. Sovereign Metals (July 2026) reported Rio declined an option to operate the Kasiya project, suggesting selective rather than expansive M&A.

**China demand.** As the largest seaborne iron ore supplier, Rio remains exposed to Chinese steel production and property-sector cycles — a risk not captured by static valuation screens.
