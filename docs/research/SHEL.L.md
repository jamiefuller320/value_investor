# Shell plc (SHEL.L) — First-Pass Research Memo

**EXECUTIVE SUMMARY**

Shell plc screens as a **Strong Buy** on quantitative value metrics: nine of 22 models pass across cheapness, dividend, GARP, and risk families, with P/E 12.8, P/B 1.4, yield 3.8%, and debt/equity 43%. The investment case rests on a globally integrated supermajor trading below historical earnings power, returning substantial cash via dividends and buybacks, whilst retaining a balance sheet that screens as manageable. The central debate is whether normalised mid-cycle cash generation supports today’s valuation after revenue and margins have normalised sharply from the 2022 commodity peak, and whether portfolio simplification (including renewables divestments) signals disciplined capital allocation or a retreat from the energy transition. Conviction on the screen signal is tempered by cyclicality, transition risk, and thin fundamental news flow.

---

**INVESTMENT THESIS**

Shell fits a classic value-investor profile: cheap on earnings and book, a meaningful dividend yield, reasonable leverage on screen, and multiple GARP-style passes (Lynch PEG, Neff PEGY, Magic Formula, Acquirer’s Multiple). The business quality underpinning the screen is that of a scaled integrated oil and gas operator with diversified upstream, downstream, and trading earnings, plus recurring shareholder distributions.

Quantitative support is broad: passes include Graham Enterprising, FCF Yield, Dividend Growth, Dreman Contrarian, and Financial Health, with sector-relative composite score 81% versus 62% overall. ROE of 10.7% is modest for a cyclical peak but acceptable at a sub-13x multiple. Financials confirm sustained cash generation: operating cash flow of $42.9bn in 2025 and free cash flow of $23.9bn, after capex of $18.9bn, with $8.5bn dividends and $13.9bn buybacks returned to shareholders.

For a value investor, Shell offers **earnings yield plus capital return** at a discount to book-driven fair value implied by P/B 1.4, with the screen flagging neutral timing (RSI 57, price roughly in line with the 200-day moving average). The gap between screen conviction (34%) and model breadth (9/22) suggests opportunity, but warrants fundamental validation rather than blind reliance on the signal alone.

**FINANCIAL REVIEW**

*Note: Income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow data are available for 2022–2025. The 2021 income statement and balance sheet entries in the source file are incomplete; no full five-year income or balance-sheet series is available.*

**Revenue and margins**

Total revenue fell from $381.3bn (2022) to $316.6bn (2023), $284.3bn (2024), and $266.9bn (2025)—a cumulative decline of roughly 30% from the post-Ukraine-invasion peak, consistent with normalising oil and gas prices. Operating income compressed from $63.1bn (2022) to $30.7bn (2023), then stabilised near $30.0bn (2024) and $27.6bn (2025). Operating margin moved from ~16.5% (2022) to ~9.7–10.6% (2023–2025). Net income followed a similar arc: $42.3bn (2022), $19.4bn (2023), $16.1bn (2024), and $17.8bn (2025); diluted EPS was $5.71, $2.85, $2.53, and $3.00 respectively. EBITDA declined from $92.6bn (2022) to $56.7bn (2025) but remains substantial.

**Leverage and balance sheet**

Total debt has trended lower: $83.8bn (2022) → $81.5bn (2023) → $77.1bn (2024) → $75.6bn (2025). Net debt was $16.1bn (2022), $15.5bn (2023), $10.5bn (2024), and $17.1bn (2025)—the 2025 uptick reflects lower cash ($29.6bn vs $37.8bn in 2024) rather than higher gross debt. Stockholders’ equity eased from $190.5bn (2022) to $174.4bn (2025), partly reflecting buybacks and distributions. Total assets declined from $443.0bn to $370.4bn. Tangible book value was $164.8bn (2022) vs $147.7bn (2025). Defined benefit pension obligations were ~$10.0bn (2022–2024) and $5.1bn (2025); non-current pension and post-retirement liabilities were $7.3–7.5bn across the period.

**Cash generation and capital allocation**

Operating cash flow: $68.4bn (2022), $54.2bn (2023), $54.7bn (2024), $42.9bn (2025). Free cash flow: $45.8bn, $31.2bn, $35.1bn, and $23.9bn over the same period. Capex has been disciplined at $22.6bn (2022), $23.0bn (2023), $19.6bn (2024), and $18.9bn (2025). Shell has consistently returned cash: dividends of $7.4–8.7bn annually and buybacks of $13.9–18.4bn (2022–2025). Financing cash outflows of $35.8–41.9bn per year reflect this shareholder-return programme alongside net debt repayment.

**Assessment**

The balance sheet remains investment-grade in character: moderate net leverage, ample liquidity, and a declining gross debt trend. Earnings and FCF have normalised from the 2022 windfall but remain robust in absolute terms. The five-year picture (where data exist) is one of **post-cycle normalisation with sustained distributions**, not distress—supporting the value screen’s cheapness and dividend passes, though not the peak-earnings multiples of 2022.

**RISKS AND RED FLAGS**

**Cyclical and commodity exposure.** Revenue, EBITDA, and FCF remain highly correlated with oil and gas prices. The 2022–2025 revenue decline illustrates earnings volatility that P/E screens may understate at mid-cycle.

**Energy transition and portfolio risk.** Sale of Sprng Energy to Aditya Birla (July 2026, ~$1.8bn including debt) signals ongoing portfolio pruning; investors must judge whether divesting renewables assets improves returns or narrows optionality in decarbonisation.

**Regulatory and litigation.** UK/EU climate disclosure, windfall taxes, and methane rules can compress margins and capex flexibility. Geopolitical disruption (Middle East, Ukraine energy infrastructure) affects both price and operational risk.

**Governance and capital allocation.** Aggressive buybacks ($13.9bn in 2025) shrink equity whilst net debt rose in 2025; sustainable only if mid-cycle FCF holds. Executive share sales (May 2026) are routine but worth monitoring alongside buyback pace.

**Pension and provisions.** Long-term provisions ($21.4–23.8bn) and pension liabilities, though manageable, are material off-balance-sheet commitments not fully captured by simple D/E screens.

**Competitive positioning.** Peers (BP, Chevron, TotalEnergies) compete on returns, transition strategy, and shareholder yield; Shell’s integrated model is a moat, but not immunity from sector re-rating on oil price moves.

**Data limitation.** Incomplete 2021 financials in the source file prevent a full five-year trend analysis for revenue and balance sheet; conclusions rely on 2022–2025.
