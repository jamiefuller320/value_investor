## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Barratt Redrow is the UK's largest listed housebuilder following the Barratt–Redrow merger, trading at a deep discount to book (P/B 0.5) with a headline dividend yield of 6.1%, which has triggered a quantitative **Strong Buy** across cheapness, dividend, GARP, and risk factor families. Reported earnings remain well below the 2022–23 cycle peak, with operating margin at 5.1% in FY2025 versus 13.3% in FY2023, and free cash flow has collapsed to £8.7m as inventory absorbs working capital. The central investment debate is whether sub-book valuation and sector-leading scale adequately compensate for cyclical housing weakness, rising build costs, merger integration, and a potential £4bn+ competition class action. Timing is neutral (RSI 58, below the 200-day moving average), and screen conviction is low at 34% given the signal is only one week old.

## INVESTMENT THESIS

The quantitative screen identifies Barratt Redrow as a classic deep-value, income-oriented opportunity: **P/B 0.51**, **P/E 18.7**, **6.1% yield**, and **D/E ~3%**, passing Schloss Low P/B, Deep Value, Lynch PEG, Neff PEGY, High Dividend Yield, Dividend Growth, Composite Value, and Financial Health models (8/22; composite score 69%, sector-relative 75%). For a value investor, the hook is tangible: £7.9bn of common equity against £11.5bn of total assets, with long-term debt stable at £200m and net cash of roughly £715m after deducting total debt of £255m from cash of £970m.

Business quality rests on scale and land bank. FY2025 revenue of **£5.58bn** exceeds pre-merger standalone levels, inventory stands at **£8.34bn** (finished goods £5.25bn; WIP £3.09bn), and the enlarged group retains FTSE 100 status with ongoing share buybacks (£50m tranche completed per news flow). Q3 FY2026 trading commentary (April 2026) cited reservation rates **6.3% above prior year**, suggesting demand resilience despite sector derating.

However, the screen's **ROE of 2.8%** and **negative FCF of -£77m** flag that cheapness reflects depressed earnings power, not hidden operational excellence. The GARP passes likely lean on low PEG/PEGY denominators after the profit collapse rather than growth acceleration. Net income of **£186m** (diluted EPS **13.3p**) in FY2025 is roughly one-third of FY2023's **£530m** (52.6p), so the value case is a cyclical/normalisation bet: if margins recover toward mid-cycle levels and inventory converts to completions, book value and dividend cover should improve materially. At current prices, the market is pricing permanent impairment; the screen argues that is excessive given balance-sheet strength and sector leadership.

Revenue was broadly flat in FY2022–23, then fell **22%** to £4.17bn in FY2024—consistent with a weak market and merger-transition distortions—before rebounding **34%** to £5.58bn in FY2025 as the combined entity scaled. Profitability tells a sharper story: operating income more than halved from the FY2023 peak, and gross profit margin compressed from **18.3%** (£975m on £5.32bn revenue) to **14.1%** (£785m on £5.58bn). FY2024 was the trough (operating margin **4.2%**; net income **£114m**). FY2025 shows partial recovery but remains far below mid-cycle earnings power.

SG&A rose to **£503m** in FY2025 from **£271m** in FY2023, reflecting the enlarged cost base. Interest expense increased steadily from **£26m** (FY2022) to **£63m** (FY2025), partly offset by interest income of **£35m**.

### Leverage and balance sheet

Total debt is modest and stable: **£254m** (FY2022), **£250m** (FY2023), **£243m** (FY2024), **£255m** (FY2025), of which long-term debt is consistently **£200m**. Common stockholders' equity grew from **£5.63bn** (FY2022) to **£7.87bn** (FY2025), with tangible book value at **£6.29bn** after **£1.17bn** of goodwill (up from **£853m** pre-merger). Total assets expanded from **£8.21bn** to **£11.55bn**.

The balance sheet is land-heavy: inventory rose from **£5.29bn** (FY2022) to **£8.34bn** (FY2025), driving working capital from **£5.18bn** to **£7.27bn**. Cash declined from **£1.35bn** (FY2022) to **£970m** (FY2025). Current provisions increased materially to **£783m** (FY2025) versus **£265m** (FY2022), and long-term provisions to **£588m** (FY2025). Defined pension benefit liability is small at **£4.2m** (FY2025). Overall leverage is low, but asset quality is cyclically sensitive given inventory concentration.

Share count dilution is notable: diluted average shares rose from **1,034m** (FY2022) to **1,398m** (FY2025), reflecting the all-share merger, which mechanically suppresses EPS and ROE.

Dividend payments have exceeded FCF for two consecutive years, which aligns with screen yield appeal but raises sustainability questions if margins do not recover.

## RISKS AND RED FLAGS

**Cyclical and operational:** UK housebuilding remains acutely cyclical. FY2024–25 margin and FCF data confirm the downcycle is biting despite FY2025 revenue recovery. Sector news (June 2026) notes housebuilders are the worst-performing FTSE 350 industrial group, down ~26% year-to-date. Rising build costs—linked in April 2026 coverage to energy/material inflation—add further margin pressure; management flagged FY2026–27 as "more uncertain."

**Regulatory and legal:** A potential **£4–4.5bn class action** (June 2026) alleging price-sharing among major housebuilders, including Barratt Redrow, requires Competition Appeal Tribunal approval but represents a material tail risk not captured in quantitative screens. Planning reform and affordable-housing obligations remain ongoing policy overhangs.

**Merger integration:** The Barratt–Redrow combination (completed 2024–25 per news flow) introduces integration risk, goodwill of **£1.17bn**, enlarged SG&A, and cultural/operational alignment challenges. FY2025 share-count dilution and elevated inventory may partly reflect merger mechanics rather than organic growth.

**Governance and management:** CEO succession announced March 2026 (retirement; infrastructure-sector replacement). CFO Rebecca Napier appointed June 2026. EY appointed external auditor July 2026 following competitive tender. Leadership transitions during a cyclical trough add execution uncertainty.

**Earnings quality:** Screen ROE of **2.8%** signals weak current return on the enlarged equity base. Negative trailing FCF (**-£77m** per screen) contradicts the dividend-yield appeal. Dividend paid well in excess of FY2025 FCF.

**Pension:** Defined benefit liability is immaterial (**£4.2m**); not a meaningful red flag on available data.

**Inventory risk:** £8.34bn of inventory (~72% of total assets) exposes the group to writedowns if land values or completion rates deteriorate.
