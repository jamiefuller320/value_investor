# Barratt Redrow plc (BTRW.L) — Research memo

_Version 1 · Updated 2026-07-13T08:04:18.673010+00:00 · Mode: initial_

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Barratt Redrow is the UK's largest listed housebuilder following the Barratt–Redrow merger, and the quantitative screen flags it as a Strong Buy on cheapness (P/B 0.5), dividend yield (6.1%), GARP, and balance-sheet risk metrics, with a sector-relative composite score of 73%. The valuation hook is a sub-book, high-yield cyclical trading well below its 200-day moving average, but earnings and margins have collapsed from 2022–23 peaks and free cash flow has nearly dried up. The central debate is whether this is a classic asset-backed value entry ahead of a housing/policy recovery, or a value trap given sector-wide derating, integration risk, and a potential £4bn+ competition lawsuit. Conviction on the screen signal is low (33%) and timing is neutral, so the case rests on balance-sheet optionality rather than near-term earnings momentum.

## INVESTMENT THESIS
For a value investor, Barratt Redrow offers a rare combination of sub-book valuation, a 6.1% dividend yield, and minimal financial leverage (D/E ~3%), passing eight of 22 screen models across all four value families: cheapness (Schloss Low P/B, Deep Value), dividend (High Dividend Yield, Dividend Growth), GARP (Lynch PEG, Neff PEGY), and risk (Composite Value, Financial Health). The screen's P/B of 0.53 versus tangible book of £6.3bn (FY2025) implies the market is discounting land banks and scale in a sector trading at multi-year lows. Post-merger revenue rebounded to £5.58bn in FY2025 (+34% vs FY2024), suggesting the enlarged platform retains top-line scale. However, the same screen shows ROE of just 2.8% and negative trailing FCF (−£77m), signalling that cheapness reflects depressed profitability rather than hidden quality. The Strong Buy case is therefore a balance-sheet and yield play on UK housing normalisation, not a growth compounder; business quality has deteriorated materially since FY2023, and the screen's "new" one-week signal warrants patience rather than conviction sizing.

## FINANCIAL REVIEW
**Note:** `financials_annual.json` provides four fiscal years (2022–2025), not five.

**Revenue** peaked at £5.32bn in FY2023, fell to £4.17bn in FY2024 (likely merger/accounting base effects), and recovered to £5.58bn in FY2025. The FY2024 trough coincided with net income collapsing to £114m from £530m in FY2023.

**Margins** compressed sharply. Gross profit margin fell from 18.3% (FY2023: £975m on £5.32bn revenue) to 12.2% (FY2024: £510m on £4.17bn), partially recovering to 14.1% (FY2025: £785m on £5.58bn). Operating margin followed the same pattern: 13.3% (FY2023), 4.2% (FY2024), 5.1% (FY2025). EBITDA declined from £767m (FY2023) to £256m (FY2024), recovering to £379m (FY2025)—still roughly half the pre-merger peak.

**Leverage** remains conservative. Total debt was stable at £242–255m across the period, with long-term debt flat at £200m. Total equity rose from £5.63bn (FY2022) to £7.87bn (FY2025), reflecting the enlarged share base (diluted average shares: 1.40bn in FY2025 vs ~1.0bn prior). Net interest expense widened to £29m (FY2025) from £11m (FY2023) as interest expense rose to £63m.

**Cash generation** has weakened materially. Free cash flow was strong at £388m (FY2022) and £442m (FY2023), but fell to £89m (FY2024) and just £9m (FY2025). Operating cash flow was only £29m in FY2025, absorbed by a £137m working-capital outflow driven by a £266m inventory build (inventory rose from £5.28bn in FY2024 to £8.34bn in FY2025). Cash declined from £1.35bn (FY2022) to £970m (FY2025). Dividends paid totalled £249m in FY2025, meaning payout exceeded free cash flow.

**Balance sheet** remains asset-heavy but provisions are rising. Total assets grew to £11.55bn (FY2025) from £7.88bn (FY2024), with goodwill up to £1.17bn. Current provisions doubled to £783m (FY2025) from £378m (FY2024). Defined pension benefit is immaterial at £4.2m (FY2025). Working capital stands at £7.27bn, reflecting the sector's land-heavy model.

## RISKS AND RED FLAGS
**Cyclical/regulatory:** UK housebuilders face persistent planning, affordability, and mortgage-market headwinds; the sector has derated sharply (sector down ~26% YTD per news flow). Rising build costs and energy-price inflation pose further margin pressure, with management flagging FY2026–27 uncertainty.

**Competition/legal:** Barratt Redrow is named in a potential £4–4.5bn class action alleging price-sharing among major builders; approval from the Competition Appeal Tribunal is pending. This is a material tail risk not captured by quantitative screens.

**Integration/governance:** The Barratt–Redrow merger creates integration complexity (goodwill £1.17bn, enlarged share count, rising provisions). CEO David Thomas is retiring (March 2026), with a new infrastructure-sector CEO incoming; Rebecca Napier was appointed CFO (June 2026). Leadership transition adds execution risk.

**Earnings quality:** ROE of 2.8% and near-zero FCF in FY2025 undermine the GARP screen passes; dividend sustainability depends on land sales and balance-sheet release, not operating cash conversion.

**Pension:** Not a meaningful risk—the defined benefit obligation is £4.2m.

**Screen limitations:** Negative FCF (−£77m on screen) and depressed ROE suggest cheapness metrics may reflect cyclical trough earnings rather than normalized profitability.
